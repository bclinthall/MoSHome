MusicSheet and WebAudioGui

These two are integrated into one, but if you are only interested in one or the other, there's no need to dive into both. This really works best in Google's Chrome browser, though most of it works in Firefox too.

MusicSheet

This app lets you input multi-part music and then play it back, adjusting the volume of each part independently. It's easy to get the hang of. To get started, just navigate to the app, and select "MusicSheet Tutorial" from the menu you'll find in the gear icon at the top left.

If you sing in a choir, this is great for letting you hear just your part of a section of music, or just your part with the other parts quiter. If you are a choir director, you can enter a piece of music and then export what you've done and email it your members to import for themselves (copyright permitting, of course).

WebAudioGUI

As the name suggests, the intention here is to make a graphical user interface for the Web Audio API. You get flowchart-style graphical audio nodes. You can drag and drop connections among them and adjust their parameters in a flexible way. The tutorial here is stored as a series of instruments. Navigate to the app (like I said, it's integrated with MusicSheet, so it's all on one page), and then from the gear menu click "Open Instrument" and start with the instrument called "Tutorial 1."

Once you've got the hang of it, MusicSheet can be used as a sequencer for WebAudioGUI. If you're interested in licensing the software to use instruments or songs that you've created here on your own website, let's talk. theatetus7, you know, gmail.

PsalmSing

The idea here is to facilitate the chanting of psalms with a variety of different melodies or "tones." You can compose and save your own tones here or use a variety of tones provided. It's been a while since I've worked on this. Right now, it seems to work only in the Firefox browser. There aren't good tutorials yet, but a couple of very rough videos are available here.

If you try to compose or edit a psalm tone, enter notes by clicking where you want them on the staff, and edit them by right clicking on them.

Orbit Game

So this one deals with spheres. It is a physics demo that I might turn into a game sometime. It's fun to play with. It very accurately models planetary motion, except you can pick up and move the sun (you can't actually do that in the real world) and the planet bounces off the sun when they collide (also doesn't really happen). If you want to see the trajectory the planet is on, check the "Show Path" box. You can see that orbits are usually elliptical or hyperbolic. Try to get the planet on a non-colliding elliptical orbit (it's pretty challenging when "Show Path" is off; it's super easy if you show the trajectory and use the "pause" feature). Some fun(?) physics stats are listed.
